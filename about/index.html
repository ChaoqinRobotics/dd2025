About The Workshop

Abstract

Data-driven techniques have demonstrated significant empirical success in addressing many challenging robotic problems continuously scaling up the complexity and pushing the boundaries of robotic autonomy in the physical world. Conversely, optimal control and trajectory optimization are foundational tools that tackle smaller-scale problems, prioritizing stability, robustness, safety, and precision. A fundamental research question in robotics, particularly in the era of foundation models, is:


How can we effectively leverage the strengths of both data-driven methods and traditional control approaches to achieve agile and intelligent robotic autonomy while ensuring safety and robustness?


This workshop aims to provide an overview of recent advancements in integrating data-driven techniques with model-based optimization methods to achieve state-of-the-art robotic autonomy. The invited talks and workshop papers will cover a broad range of foundational knowledge in robotics, such as reinforcement learning and optimal control while emphasizing their application to real-world challenges. It will be highly beneficial for researchers seeking to explore cutting-edge methodologies, engineers looking for practical solutions to complex robotic challenges, and students aiming to build a solid foundation in data-driven and traditional control approaches. By bridging theory and practice, we will offer valuable insights and networking opportunities for participants at all academic and professional stages.



The Content of the Workshop


Relevance and Objectives


This workshop aims to provide an opportunity for cross-disciplinary researchers to share insights on data-driven control and optimization techniques crucial for developing next-generation autonomous robotic systems. As robots have been receiving high expectations to operate in complex and dynamic environments, the research towards autonomy has expanded beyond the robot’s stability, robustness, dissipativity, and optimality. Keywords such as agility, self-adaptability, real-time efficiency, and general-purpose functionality have captured public attention. With the growing demand for robots to perform in complex and ever-changing environments, the scope of autonomous research has broadened beyond ensuring stability, robustness, and optimal performance. There is now a strong emphasis on developing robots that can respond swiftly, adapt to new situations, operate efficiently in real-time, handle a wide range of tasks seamlessly, and ultimately achieve a general-purpose system. Such a paradigm shift has a bright future thanks to the combination of novel learning-based methods with classical control and optimization theories, including but not limited to, reinforcement learning in simulation, regression methods for modeling, neural network approximations for online solving, and Large Language Models (LLMs) in the loop. Such a paradigm shift also demands researchers and students to develop a fundamental understanding of diverse topics across different disciplines, including but not limited to reinforcement learning, machine learning, numerical optimization for control and planning, and Large Foundation Models.


This workshop aims to bring researchers who think rigorously across those emerging disciplines and to foster collaborative discussions on how to effectively use data to drive autonomy to the next level.


By bridging experts from diverse fields, this workshop aims to foster collaborative discussion and innovative research on how to use data to drive autonomy to the next level. The goal of this workshop is aligned with ICRA’s goal of discussing emerging robotics techniques.



Originality and Diversity


Existing robotics workshops in control and trajectory optimization seldom focus on autonomy with data-driven approaches. This workshop will fill in the gap by concentrating on the intersection of data-driven methods with control and optimization for robotics. Our topic selection naturally attracts a diverse range of academia and industry attention not only from the field of robotics but also from control systems, machine learning, reinforcement learning, and optimization.
